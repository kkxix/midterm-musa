Philadelphia Eviction Judgments Prediction

MUSA 5080 Final Project

AKH Consultants - Annalise Abraham, Katie Knox, and Hope Levin

2025-12-08

Policy Context

Evictions are High in Philadelphia

  • There have been 11,166 eviction filings in Philadelphia so far in 2025.
  • Over the past 12 months the eviction filing rate has been 4%.

Source: Eviction Lab

Eviction Filings vs Judgements

  • The legal process of eviction involves going to the Municipal Court, filing an eviction, a hearing is scheduled, and eventually a judgement is made.
  • A lot of filings (about half in our dataset) are settled outside of court between the landlord and tenant
  • The vast majority of filings that go on to a court judgement end up ruling in the landlord’s favor
–Source: Eviction Lab

The Eviction Filing Rate is Uneven Across Philadelphia

Problem Statement

Can we predict the number of eviction filings that will lead to judgments per census tract in Philadelphia?

  • If a is more likely to lead to a judgement it signifies areas where landlords are less willing to negotiate with a tenant outside of court
  • The tenant is more likely for the outcome to not be in their favor
  • Thus more resources and regulations should target those areas

Related Questions:

  • What are the characteristics of the census tracts with the highest share of judgements / filings?
  • How can the city better protect renters in these high risk areas?

Data Sources

  • We use observed and validated eviction filing and judgement data by Philadelphia census tract from Eviction Lab. Data is only available for 2010, 2011, 2012, and 2016.
  • ACS 2016 5-Year Data
  • Property Sales (2023-2024) - Office of Property Assessment

Our Model: Binomial Regression

Given a filing, what is the probability of judgement? –> Binary outcome (judgement or no judgement)

binomial_group_model <- glm(cbind(judgments, filings - judgments) ~ category_code_description + median_year_built + total_livable_area + factor(year) + rent_burden_rate + median_h_incomeE + familiesE + black_percent + total_pop,
                         family = binomial,
                         data = model_data)

Key Significant Variables

  • Dependant Variable: (judgments, filings - judgments) allows us to weight probability of judgement by number of filings in census tract.
  • Demographics: Rent burden, median household income, number of families, percent of balck residents, total population.
  • Housing Type: We used the most common property type per tract by category code, median year built, and total livable area.

Cross Validation

  • Train on 2010, 2011, 2012
  • Test on 2016

Results

test_data$predicted_judgments <- test_data$filings * test_data$predicted_prob_judgment

Limitations and Bias Considerations

  • Major policy change since 2016, need more up-to-date data

Recommendations for Implementation

  1. Monitor large landlords in high risk areas
  2. Dedicate rental assistance programs to high risk areas
  3. Dedicate legal aid resources to higher judgement-liklihood areas
